Andromeda is a constellation in the northern sky. It is named after Andromeda, the princess in the Greek legend of Perseus who was chained to a rock to be eaten by the sea monster Cetus. It was one of the 48 constellations listed by the 2nd century astronomer Ptolemy, and remains one of the 88 modern constellations. Andromeda is prominent in the northern sky during the fall, along with several other constellations named for characters in the Perseus myth. Several binary stars along with the Andromeda Galaxy (M31), the closest galaxy to the Milky Way and one of the brightest Messier objects, lie in Andromeda's borders.

[edit] History and mythology

Andromeda is also known as "the Chained Lady" or "the Chained Woman" in English. It was known as Mulier Catenata ("chained woman") in Latin, and al-Mar'at al Musalsalah in Arabic).[1][3] It has also been called Persea ("Perseus's wife") or Cepheis ("Cepheus's daughter").[1] All of these names reference Andromeda's role in the Greco-Roman myth of Perseus. In the myth, Cassiopeia, the queen of Ethiopia, bragged that her daughter was more beautiful than the Nereids. Offended at her remark, the nymphs petitioned Poseidon to punish Cassiopeia for her insolence, which he did by commanding the sea monster Cetus to attack Ethiopia. Andromeda's panicked father, Cepheus was told by the Oracle of Ammon that the only way to save his kingdom was to sacrifice his daughter to Cetus.[4] She was then chained to a rock by the sea, but was saved by the hero Perseus, who used the head of Medusa to turn the monster into stone and subsequently married Andromeda.[5][3] After her death, Athena placed Andromeda in the sky as a constellation to honor her.[3]

Andromeda was one of the original 48 constellations formulated by Ptolemy in his Almagest. Since then, it has remained a constellation and is currently recognized as an official constellation by the International Astronomical Union.[2][5] Part of Andromeda and most of Lacerta were taken in 1787 by German astronomer Johann Bode to form the constellation Frederici Honores (also called Friedrichs Ehre). It was designed to honor King Frederick II of Prussia and has since become obsolete.[2]

[edit] Notable features

[edit] Stars

See also: List of stars in the constellation Andromeda
  • α And (Alpheratz, Sirrah) is the brightest star in this constellation. It is a binary star with an overall apparent visual magnitude of 2.06m. It represents Andromeda's head in Western mythology, however, the star's traditional Arabic names—Alpheratz and Sirrah—mean "horse" and "navel".[4][6] This star forms an asterism known as the "Great Square of Pegasus" with three stars in Pegasus: α, β, and γ Peg. As such, the star was formerly considered to belong to both Andromeda and Pegasus, and was co-designated as "Delta Pegasi (δ Peg)", although this name is no longer formally used.
  • β And (Mirach) is located in an asterism known as the "girdle". It is 200 light years distant and of magnitude 2.1m.
  • γ And (Almach) is found at the southern tip of the constellation. It is a multiple star with contrasting colors.[6]
  • δ And is a 3rd magnitude star.
  • ι And, κ, λ, ο, and ψ And form an asterism known as "Frederik's Glory", a name derived from a former constellation (Frederici Honores).[2]
  • υ And is a binary system, of one F-type dwarf and a M-type dwarf. The primary star has a planetary system with four confirmed planets, 0.96 times, 14.57 times, 10.19 times and 1.06 the mass of Jupiter.[7]
  • ξ And (Adhil) is a binary star.
  • n And (33 And) was a former designation for M31, the Andromeda Galaxy.
  • 51 And was reassigned by Johann Bayer to neighbouring Perseus, where he designated it "Upsilon Persei (υ Per)", but it was returned to Andromeda by the International Astronomical Union.[8]
  • 54 And was a former designation for φ Per.[8]

[edit] Deep-sky objects

The most famous deep-sky object in Andromeda is the spiral galaxy named for the constellation and cataloged as Messier 31 (M31) or NGC 224.[5] The Andromeda Galaxy is one of the most distant objects visible to the naked eye at a distance of 2.2 million light-years and the largest neighboring galaxy to the Milky Way.[5] It is an enormous barred spiral galaxy much like the Milky Way, and at an approximate magnitude of 3.5, is one of the brightest deep-sky objects in the northern sky.[9][2]

The Andromeda Galaxy's two main companions, M32 and M110 (also known as NGC 221 and NGC 205, respectively) are faint elliptical galaxies that lie near it.[5][2] M32 appears superimposed on the larger galaxy in a telescopic view as hazy smudge, and M110 appears slightly larger and distinct from the larger galaxy.[5] M32 was discovered in 1749 by French astronomer Guillaume Le Gentil, and has since been found to lie closer to Earth than the Andromeda Galaxy itself.[10] M110 is classified as either a dwarf spheroidal galaxy or simply a generic elliptical galaxy. It possesses a noteworthy system of 8 globular clusters, far more than most galaxies of its size.[11]

There is one prominent planetary nebula in Andromeda: NGC 7662. Lying approximately three degrees southwest of Iota Andromedae, the "Blue Snowball Nebula" is a popular target for amateur astronomers.[5] [12] It earned its popular name because it appears as a round blue-green object in a telescope.[5]

[edit] Meteor showers

In November, the Andromedids meteor shower appears to radiate from Andromeda.[2] It peaks in mid- to late- November every year, but has a low peak rate of less than three meteors per hour.[13] Astronomers have often associated the Andromedids with Biela's Comet, a comet that was destroyed in the 19th century, but this connection is somewhat disputed.[14] Andromedid meteors are known for being very slow, and the shower itself is considered to be diffuse, as meteors can be seen coming from nearby constellations as well as Andromeda itself.[15] The Andromedids were also associated with the most spectacular meteor showers of the 19th century. The storms of 1872 and 1885 were estimated to have a peak rate of two meteors per second, prompting one Chinese astronomer to compare the meteors to falling rain.[14]

[edit] In non-Western astronomy

In Chinese astronomy, the stars of Andromeda correspond to two areas of the sky known as the Black Tortoise of the North (北方玄武, Běi Fāng Xuán Wǔ) and the White Tiger of the West (西方白虎, Xī Fāng Bái Hǔ).[citation needed]
