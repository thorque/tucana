Caelum ( /ˈsiːləm/) is a faint constellation in the southern sky, introduced in the 18th century by Nicolas Louis de Lacaille. Its name means "the chisel" in Latin, and it was formerly known as Caelum Scalptorium, "the engraver's chisel".[1] It is the eighth smallest constellation, with an area just less than that of Corona Australis.

[edit] Notable features

See also: List of stars in Caelum

Caelum is a faint constellation, having no star brighter than fourth magnitude. Its brightest star is the magnitude 4.45 α Caeli.

A small telescope splits γ Caeli into a magnitude 4.5 red giant and a magnitude 6.34 white giant. Larger telescopes are needed to see several NGC objects, all galaxies, but none brighter than magnitude 11.5 or larger than 3 arcminutes.[2]

Most notable galaxies are NGC 1571, NGC 1679 and IC 2106.[3]

[edit] Namesakes

USS Caelum (AK-106) was a United States Navy ship named after the constellation.
